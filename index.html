Historical context

The opioid crisis on a national scale can be traced in three major waves.

The first wave stems from early 1990â€™s efforts to reduce pain in patients. This stemmed from the argument that chronic and/or recurrent pain was a serious liability to productivity and quality of life, and the (now proven false) hypothesis that addiction to opioids was a rarity. Further fueling this were pharmaceutical companies advertising their prescriptions as nonaddictive. As a result, opioid prescriptions grew precipitously, and with them an increase in drug overdoses followed.

The second wave came as doctors and the government noted the uptick in prescription opioids were causing more drug overdose deaths, and as such began procedures to combat them, which came in two parts:

  1. First, pharmaceutical companies created a new formulation of the pill to extend the release window and resist to being crushed into powder.
  2. Secondly, doctors became more cautious with their prescriptions, due both to laws by the government and their own increasing knowledge about the addictiveness of such drugs.

Due to these efforts, prescription drugs alone stopped causing as many deaths. However, more and more people started turning to heroin to get the same effect, and thus the death toll due to heroin began to rise.

The third wave, supplementing the second wave, was the addition of synthetic opioids, such as fentanyl. These drugs were highly potent, and overdosing on them very easy. This is the most recent type of drug to be noted, and is notable for its sharp increase in recent years.
